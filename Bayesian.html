Frequentist AND Bayesian A/B Testing¶

In this article, we will use the frequentist and bayesian approach to A/B testing to evaluate a synthetic website's Click Through Rate (CTR)

First let's generate some data. We will assume that variant A and B has a true CTR of 0.03 and 0.05 respectively. And, like in most realistic cases, we'll assume that we've only allocated 20% of the traffic to website variant B.

We're now ready to generate some data using bernoulli distributions: $B(0.03)$ of size 8,000 and $B(0.05)$ of size 2,000.

Note: In practice, we're not given the true CTR's and the true $\Delta$

Business Question:¶

  • Does Variant B performing better in terms of CTR happened by chance?

Frequentist Approach - $\chi^2$ Test¶

First, we need to compute a theoretical CTR under the assumption that there are no difference between performance. 

Theoretical CTR: 0.0351

Next, we'll use the theoretical CTR to get the theoretical clicks on the 2 variants.

Theoretical Clicks on A: 280
Theoretical Clicks on B: 70

Now we can compute how likely our data is, given the assumption that there are no difference between performance. The p-value is less than the usual $\alpha=0.05$ and hence suggest that there is indeed a difference between performance. 

p-value: 1.114494950251193e-06

Bayesian Approach¶

Pros:

  • You can easily incorporate domain information.
  • Much more intuitive.
  • Can easily express uncertainty.

Cons:

  • Unless you use conjugate priors, it can be harder to solve.
  • Includes intergrals.

Luckily, we have Pymc3 which will do all the heavy lifting for us. It has a set of sampling algorithms to calculate the posterior distribution. In this post, we'll use the default NUTS. (No U-Turn-Sampler)

Given your domain knowledge, you know that CTRs are unlikely to be higher than 1%. Hence, we assign a uniform distribution in the interval $(0,0.1).$ Using Pymc3, we'll get the Posterior Distribution for the CTR of variant A and B, and their delta.

Posterior Distributions¶

  • Red lines indicates the true values
  • Since we have less data for variant B, notice that it has a "fatter" posterior distribution. Hence, it suggests that we are more uncertain for our estimate.
  • The distribution for delta concists mostly of negative values. And hence this suggest that indeed performace for variant B is better.
